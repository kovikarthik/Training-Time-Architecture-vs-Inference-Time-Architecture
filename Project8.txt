(Why One Accelerator Cannot Efficiently Do Both)
1. Project Overview and Description
Modern AI systems are often built on hardware platforms originally optimized for training workloads ,
then repurposed for inference . This assumption—that “fast training hardware implies fast
inference”—is increasingly false, especially for large language models (LLMs).
In this project, students will systematically compare training-time and inference-time
architectures , focusing on how dif ferences in:
Parallelism structure
Memory behavior
Numerical precision
Control flow
Fault tolerance
Latency vs. throughput objectives
lead to fundamentally different optimal architectures .
Rather than designing a single accelerator , students must analyze, contrast, and justify
architectural divergence , and then propose separate, specialized architectures  (or explain why a
unified design fails).

Training optimizes for throughput and scale.
Inference optimizes for latency , efficiency , and predictability .
Key industry implications:
1. Misaligned Hardware Investment
Training-optimized accelerators are often underutilized during inference.
Inference workloads expose memory and control inef ficiencies.
2. Different Bottlenecks
Training: compute-bound, communication-heavy
Inference: memory-bound, control-dominated
3. Economic Reality
Inference typically dominates total cost of ownership (TCO).
Architectural inef ficiency at inference scale is extremely expensive.
Students learn to distinguish architectural myths from measured reality .
